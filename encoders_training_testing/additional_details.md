# Fine-Tuning Details

We compare two Portuguese biomedical language models, MediAlbertina and BioBERTpt, fine-tuned under identical conditions against generative baselines. The task was formulated as BIO sequence labelling over subword tokens, yielding nine output labels: one *O* tag plus *B-* and *I-* prefixes for each of the four entity classes. To handle the length of the clinical notes, a sliding-window chunking strategy was applied during both training and inference, dividing each document into overlapping windows of 512 tokens with a stride of 128 tokens. At inference time, logit scores from overlapping windows were averaged per token before the argmax decoding step, ensuring that predictions in overlap regions reflect the full surrounding context from both adjoining windows.

Two complementary strategies were applied to address the significant imbalance between entity classes. First, inverse frequency class weights were applied to the cross-entropy loss, with the *O*-class weight capped at 0.25 times the mean non-*O* weight to prevent over-suppression of entity predictions, and with every *I-X* weight forced equal to its corresponding *B-X* weight to prevent the model from learning to truncate multi-token spans prematurely. Second, document-level oversampling was applied to the training set, where every document containing at least one Medication Allergies with negative polarity span was duplicated four times, increasing the effective exposure to this minority class without modifying the validation or test distributions.

Both models were fine-tuned using the Hugging Face Transformers library for up to 20 epochs with the AdamW optimiser, a learning rate of 2 x 10^(-5), weight decay of 0.01, a cosine learning rate schedule with a warmup ratio of 0.10, and a maximum gradient norm of 1.0. The effective batch size was 16, with 4 samples per device and gradient accumulation over 4 steps. Training was performed in bfloat16 mixed precision. Early stopping was applied with a patience of 3 epochs, monitored on validation F1, and only the best checkpoint was retained.

To assess model performance, we compare the fine-tuned NER models against generative LLM-based approaches. We include both an open-weight model and a closed-weight model to better evaluate the trade-off between different deployment scenarios. Closed-weight API-based models may offer stronger general capabilities, but they are less suitable for ER settings due to privacy and data governance concerns. In contrast, open-weight models can be deployed locally within hospital infrastructure, making them more appropriate for privacy-sensitive clinical environments, although potentially with lower overall performance. For this setting, we selected Gemini (*gemini-2.5-flash-lite*) as the closed-weight model and Gemma 4 (*gemma-4-31B-it*) as the open-weight model. To ensure a fair comparison and obtain structured outputs from both generative models, we used LangExtract.

Results are reported under two span matching criteria. *Exact match* requires identical character-level start and end indices between predicted and gold spans, while *relaxed match* (IoU ≥ 0.50) accepts predictions whose character-level overlap with the gold span meets a minimum intersection-over-union threshold of 50%, accommodating minor boundary differences. In both cases, evaluation is conducted in *joint-extraction-and-polarity* mode, where missed and spurious spans are penalised in addition to polarity errors.
